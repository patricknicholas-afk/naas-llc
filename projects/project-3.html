Case Study

Anheuser-Busch

Building a unified design system for one of the world's largest beverage companies, creating consistency across 500+ brands while preserving each brand's distinct identity.

Role Design Systems Lead
Timeline 14 Months
Platform Web & Mobile
Scale 500+ Brands

The Problem

Anheuser-Busch InBev operates 500+ brands across 150+ markets, from Budweiser and Stella Artois to regional craft labels. Each brand had its own design team, agency relationships, and digital presence, resulting in a fragmented ecosystem where every new campaign or digital touchpoint was built from scratch.

The cost was staggering: duplicated design effort across teams, inconsistent user experiences that eroded brand trust, and a 6-8 week average timeline to launch a new brand microsite. Marketing teams were spending more time negotiating with agencies about button styles than crafting strategy.

I was brought in to lead the design system initiative, creating a shared foundation that could serve the entire brand portfolio while respecting each brand's unique visual identity.

Research & Discovery

Before designing anything, I needed to understand the full scope of the problem: how many teams were affected, what patterns already existed, and where the biggest inefficiencies lived.

Component Audit

I led a comprehensive audit across 40 brand properties, cataloging every unique component, interaction pattern, and design decision. The results were eye-opening: we found 23 different button styles, 14 navigation patterns, and 8 distinct form implementations doing essentially the same thing.

Stakeholder Interviews

I interviewed 25+ stakeholders across brand teams, engineering, marketing, and agency partners. The tension was clear:

  • Brand teams feared a design system would flatten their brand's personality into generic templates
  • Engineering wanted shared code but couldn't adopt it if every brand needed custom overrides
  • Marketing needed faster launch times but couldn't sacrifice brand authenticity
  • Agencies resisted standardization because bespoke work justified their retainers
  • Leadership wanted consistency and cost reduction without understanding the technical tradeoffs

The Core Insight

The breakthrough was realizing that brand differentiation lived in a small number of high-impact properties (color, typography, imagery, tone) while structural patterns (layout, navigation, forms, accessibility) could, and should, be shared. The system needed to be a platform, not a template.

Design Process

Token Architecture

I designed a three-tier token system that became the foundation of the entire design system:

  • Tier 1: Global tokens: Spacing scale, grid system, breakpoints, elevation, motion curves. Shared across every brand, never overridden.
  • Tier 2: Brand tokens: Color palettes, typography stacks, border radius preferences, imagery guidelines. Each brand defines its own set.
  • Tier 3: Component tokens: Map semantic roles (primary-action, surface, text-secondary) to brand token values. This is where brand personality meets structural consistency.

This architecture meant a "Button" component could look distinctly Budweiser or distinctly Stella Artois while sharing identical accessibility, interaction, and responsive behavior.

Component Library

I designed 45 core components with full state coverage: default, hover, active, focused, disabled, loading, error, and empty states. Each component was documented with usage guidelines, do/don't examples, and accessibility requirements.

Governance Model

A design system without governance dies. I established a contribution model with clear criteria for when to extend the system vs. when to create a brand-specific component, a review process for new additions, and quarterly audits to catch drift.

The Solution

The final design system, internally called "Brew," shipped as both a Figma library and a React component library, with documentation hosted on an internal platform.

Brand Theming Engine

The centerpiece was the theming engine. Brand teams fill out a configuration file with their brand tokens (colors, fonts, radius, imagery) and the entire component library adapts automatically. A new brand can go from zero to a fully themed, accessible component set in under 2 hours.

Page Templates

I designed 12 page templates covering the most common brand needs: product landing pages, campaign microsites, age gates, store locators, and event pages. Each template is assembled from system components and pre-wired with analytics and accessibility compliance.

Documentation & Onboarding

The documentation site includes interactive component playgrounds, brand token previews, copy-paste code snippets, and a decision-tree tool for choosing the right component. I designed this as a product, not just reference docs; because adoption depends on discoverability.

Validation

Pilot Program

We launched with 3 pilot brands: Budweiser (global flagship), a mid-tier regional brand, and a recently acquired craft label. This gave us validation across the full spectrum of brand complexity and team capability.

Accessibility Audit

Every component was tested against WCAG 2.1 AA standards. I worked with an accessibility specialist to ensure color contrast, keyboard navigation, screen reader support, and focus management were built into the system, not bolted on after the fact.

Developer Experience Testing

I ran usability testing with front-end developers to validate the component API, documentation clarity, and theming workflow. The biggest iteration was simplifying the token override syntax: developers needed fewer steps to customize components without breaking the system's constraints.

Impact

The design system rolled out across AB InBev's digital portfolio over 14 months and fundamentally changed how the organization builds digital experiences.

  • 75% reduction in microsite launch time: from 6-8 weeks to under 2 weeks
  • 45 shared components with full state coverage, adopted across 12 brand teams
  • 100% WCAG 2.1 AA compliance built into every component
  • $2.3M estimated annual savings in reduced agency and duplicate design effort
  • 3 pilot brands to 12 active brands within 6 months of launch

The three-tier token architecture became the reference model for AB InBev's broader digital transformation, with the pattern being evaluated for their mobile app portfolio as well.

What I Learned

The hardest part of a design system isn't the components; it's the politics. Adoption is a change management problem, not a design problem. The token architecture was the technical breakthrough, but the governance model and stakeholder alignment were what made it stick. A design system that nobody uses is just a Figma file.
