Case Study

Audible

Intentionally displaying back-end metadata to meet the customer mindset, aligning purpose and functionality across Audible's product detail page for 500K+ titles worldwide.

Role Senior Product Designer
Timeline 12 Months
Platform Web Application
Company Amazon / Audible

The Scenario

Business

Customer data indicated that Audible's product detail page (PDP) functions primarily as a navigational touchpoint. Listeners weren't landing on a title to confirm a decision they'd already made; they were using the PDP to discover their next great listen. That changes everything about what the page needs to do.

Back-end metadata is rich, but how it surfaces to the customer determines whether that data drives discovery or creates noise. The opportunity was to display that metadata with intention, mapping it directly to how customers think and browse rather than how the system stores it.

Customer

Tag display on the PDP was too widely spread, with insufficient depth in any single dimension. Customers encountered a broad scatter of labels without the breadth to meaningfully orient within a genre, theme, or mood. The result was a page that looked informative but failed to move people forward.

Technical Context

Understanding how metadata is structured at the back end was essential to making intentional decisions about what surfaces to the customer and how. Three systems shaped the design constraints.

Ingestion Model

Metadata enters the Audible system through a structured ingestion pipeline that normalizes and validates submissions from publishers across global markets. The model governs how data is accepted, cleaned, and routed before it ever reaches the customer-facing catalog.

World Tree Verticals

Audible's taxonomy is organized around a hierarchical "World Tree" of content verticals, providing a structured L1 through L4 classification framework across genre, theme, subject, and mood. This tree defines the inheritance model for tags and determines which labels a title can legitimately carry.

Authoritative vs. Subjective Data

Not all metadata is equal. Authoritative data (narrator, series, publisher, runtime) is factual and verifiable. Subjective and objective data (thematic tags, mood labels, listener-contributed attributes) requires editorial judgment to apply consistently. Designing for both types meant building distinct display patterns for each.

Challenges and Risks

  • Over-saturation of tags: surfacing too much information creates cognitive overload rather than useful orientation
  • Tag duplication: overlapping labels from different taxonomy levels produce redundancy on the page without adding discovery value

PDP tags audit

Audible's existing tag taxonomy contained over 400 loosely defined labels with no consistent hierarchy or inheritance model. A full audit mapped each tag to a structured L1–L4 world tree, surfacing gaps and redundancies across genre, theme, and mood dimensions.

Recommendations

Two strategic directions were developed in response to the scenario, each targeting a different layer of the discovery problem.

Ranked Strategies

Rather than displaying all available tags at equal weight, ranked strategies prioritize metadata based on customer relevance signals. Tags most correlated with navigation behavior and conversion surface first, while lower-signal attributes are deprioritized or omitted entirely. This directly addresses the oversaturation risk by letting data quality govern what appears, not what the system happens to have.

P13N Strategies

Personalization strategies adapt the tag display to the individual listener's context: browsing history, genre affinities, and listening patterns inform which metadata dimensions are surfaced on a given PDP. A listener who consistently browses by narrator sees narrator prominence; a listener driven by theme sees thematic tags lead. The page meets the customer where they already are.

Expected Results

Both strategies were expected to increase conversion rate (CVR) through more accurate and meaningful informational tags, producing a more optimized and relevant browsing experience for listeners across all catalog segments.

The Solution

The design solution centered on a single principle: surface back-end metadata in a way that mirrors the customer's mental model, not the system's data structure. Every display decision mapped back to one of the two strategic directions.

Intentional Tag Hierarchy

Tags are displayed in a ranked order derived from customer navigation data. The most discovery-relevant dimensions lead; supporting attributes follow. The hierarchy adapts to title type, ensuring a non-fiction title surfaces differently from a thriller without requiring manual editorial override.

World Tree-Informed Display

The World Tree vertical structure was used to deduplicate and consolidate tags before they reach the page. Child tags that are fully implied by a parent tag are suppressed, eliminating the redundancy that previously created visual noise without adding navigational value.

Personalized Metadata Surfaces

For logged-in listeners, the P13N strategy surfaces the metadata dimension most relevant to their listening history. The underlying tag set remains consistent; the order and prominence shift to match the individual. This required close collaboration with the recommendations engineering team to expose the right signals at the right time.

Validation

Phased Rollout

Given the critical nature of the pipeline (it feeds Audible's customer-facing catalog), we rolled out changes incrementally. Each phase ran in parallel with the existing system for 2 weeks, with operators able to toggle between old and new. This de-risked the transition and gave us real usage data before fully cutting over.

Operator Feedback Sessions

I ran bi-weekly feedback sessions with operators throughout the rollout. The most significant iteration was reworking the batch editor's confirmation flow; operators needed more explicit "before/after" previews before committing changes that affected hundreds of titles simultaneously.

Metrics Tracking

We instrumented every workflow to measure time-on-task, error rates, and throughput. These metrics were shared transparently with the operations team; they became partners in optimization, not just recipients of a new tool.

Contributions

The work produced five distinct deliverables, each designed to serve a specific team and create lasting value beyond the immediate project scope.

For Development

Back-End Data Strategies

New back-end strategies to improve data structures, giving engineering a clear framework for how metadata should be organized and prioritized before it ever reaches the front end.

For Development & Design

Design System Components

Scalable, reusable design system components built to handle the full range of metadata display states, ensuring consistency across the catalog and reducing one-off implementation work.

For Stakeholder Alignment

PDP Architecture

A data-driven customer mental model architecture for the PDP, providing stakeholders with a principled framework for evaluating future optimizations against actual browsing behavior.

For UX Research

Research Frameworks

A usability interview guide and customer mental model frameworks, giving the research team the tools to continue validating metadata display decisions with real listeners over time.

For Design & UX Research

User Task Flows

User task flows mapped across major information architecture states, creating a shared reference for both design and research to evaluate how customers navigate through the catalog.
